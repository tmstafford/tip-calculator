North America:

Canada

Tipping in Canada generally follows the same rules as the United States. However, the hourly rates for service employees are typically higher in Canada than they are in the US, so things like tip jars on counters are not likely to be seen, and the extra cash tip isn't as necessary.

Mexico

Mexico's tipping expectation is generally around 10-15%. If great service is offered, the tip should be bumped up to around 18-20%. Usually, it is recommended to stick with typical US tipping habits if traveling to Mexico.

South America:

Costa Rica

A majority of bar and restaurant bills in Costa Rica already include an explicitly-stated 10% service charge. That 10% will still be part of the charge, even if the bill says "service not included". An additional tip on top of the service charge is not required and is not considered "custom practice", however the gesture is always welcome and appreciated.

Argentina

A 10-15% tip is expected at restaurants and bars in Argentina. Also, the service staff in Argentina prefer to recieve their tips in cash and in their local currrency, the Argentine Peso (ARS). Currently 1 Argentine Peso = 0.012 US Dollars.

Brazil

There is often a 10% service charge added to bar and restaurant bills in Brazil. If there is no service charge included, 15% is the standard gratuity charge. Something important to know before tipping in Brazil: tipping in US dollars is considered rude. Cash tips in Brazilian Real are appreciated, but make sure to get your money exchanged before getting served in Brazil. Currently 1 Brazilian Real = 0.19 US Dollars.

Europe:

United Kingdom (UK)

A service charge is included at the majority of businesses in the UK. If not, a "suggested" service charge will be referenced on the bill that can be adjusted as you see fit. Typically, a 10-15% tip is considered apporpriate.

France

In France, service is generally included in the bill. Check your bill for the phrase "service compris", if it's there you'll know that service is included in the total. But the French service staff won't turn down a "pourboire", a French colloquial term for tips, so rounding off your bill or leaving a few extra euros will be just fine.

Italy

There's a custom known as "pane e coperto" in Italy, where guests pay a cover charge before entering a bar or restaurant, however it is becoming less and less common, even outlawed in certain regions. If you don't encounter "pane e coperto" in Italy, there will most likely be a "servizio", or a service charge automatically added to the bill. Tipping in addition to the service charge isn't expected, but rounding up the bill is seen as a polite gesture in Italy.

Spain

In recent years, the trend of tipping at least 10% at higher-end restaurants in Spain has emerged. Generally, gratuity is included in the bill at Spanish restaurants, and rounding up your bill is a good way to tip here. Extra tips are not required or custom in Spain.

Asia:

Israel

Restaurants in Israel usually include gratuity in the bill. Nowadays, there is an informal but widely-practiced custom that encourages leaving a 1-shekel tip for each member of your party. Most businesses will accept US currency, the the shekel is preferred for cash tips. Currently, 1 shekel = 0.31 US Dollars.

United Arab Emirates

In the United Arab Emirates, the US tipping custom applies in most circumstances. The widely considered norm is 15-20% gratuity for service. In Dubai, the government requires all bars, restaurants, and hotels to add a 10% service charge to bills. With the service charge, simply round out the bill with a few dirhams. There are some circumstances in the UAE where tipping is considered rude. The most notable example involves tipping bathroom attendants, so be sure to check for further standards.

India

Tipping isn't required in most regions of India, but is deemed courteous. In larger cities and tourist areas, tipping is becoming more expected and many businesses have adopted the practice of adding a service charge to the bill. If there is no explicit service charge added, the standard tip is around 10%.

China

Tipping in China isn't usually considered a part of Chinese culture, however it is becoming more expected at trendy urban restaurants. It's very possible that this practice could become more widespread in the future, but the majority of restaurants in China won't expect it.

Japan

Tipping in Japan must be done with caution, since leaving a cash tip on the table or handing money to a service worker can be considered rude. Japanese service workers don't expect tips, but if you feel the need to leave one, do so respectfully. This would entail putting some yen in an envelope and handing the concealed tip to the server.

Thailand

The locals in Thailand will always be happy and appreciate a small tip, but tipping isn't necessary at all. Although tips are not expected, if you want to leave gratuity, simply round up the bill.

Vietnam

Tipping in Vietnam is considered optional. There is an informal expectation of about 5-10% for good service, particularly in major travel cities. So 5-10% is the norm, but for absolutely excellent service, 10-15% is expected.

Africa:

Egypt

Gratuity will be included in your bill at most Egyptian restaurants. However, it is customary to leave an additional 5-10% on top of that. For cash tips in Egypt, many businesses prefer US dollars rather than local currency.

South Africa

A 10-15% tip is customary in South Africa for a sit-down meal. Some businesses will include service charges on the bill, but if not, stick to the 10-15% norm. For just a drink at the bar, rounding-up the bill is perfectly acceptable.

Australia:

Australia

Tips aren't expected in Australia because the service staff receive a livable wage. If exceptional service is received, a small tip, a few dollars or just rounding-up the bill, will generally be accepted and appreciated.
